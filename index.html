The walk

Here's how the exam works, how to get your license, and how this site helps you get there.

The exam

The Customs Broker License Examination (CBLE) is offered twice a year - April and October. It's 80 multiple-choice questions in 4.5 hours. Open book. You need 75% to pass (60 out of 80).

"Open book" sounds generous until you realize the pass rate averages around 15%. The questions are designed so that knowing where to find the answer matters more than memorizing it. You're allowed to bring Title 19 CFR, the Harmonized Tariff Schedule, and ACE documents. The people who pass are fast navigators, not encyclopedias.

Path to your license

1

Study the material

Get your printed CFR and HTS. Tab them. Use this site to practice with real exam questions and learn where things live in the regulations.

2

Register for the exam

CBP opens registration a few months before each exam. Seats fill up. Register early at cbp.gov. Fee is around $390.

3

Take the exam

4.5 hours, 80 questions, open book. Bring your tabbed CFR, HTS, calculator, highlighters. Time management is everything.

4

Pass with 75%+

Results come out a few weeks later. If you pass, you move to the application phase.

5

Apply for your license

Submit CBP Form 3124 (Application for Customs Broker License). Includes background check, fingerprinting, and character references. Processing takes several months.

6

Get licensed

Once approved, you hold a national customs broker license. Maintain it with triennial status reports (every 3 years) and continuing education.

How to study

This isn't a memorization exam. It's a navigation exam. Your job is to know the material well enough to find the exact answer in your reference books within 3 minutes.

→

Weeks 1-2: Read through the CFR parts that matter most (111, 141, 142, 152, 113). Don't memorize - get familiar with the structure.

→

Weeks 3-4: Start doing practice questions in Free Study mode. When you get one wrong, look up the regulation. Tab that section.

→

Weeks 5-6: Take full practice tests (80 questions). Track your time. Identify which categories are weakest and drill those.

→

Week 7+: Speed rounds daily. Practice tests weekly. Focus on getting your lookup time under 2 minutes per question.

The goal isn't to know every regulation by heart. It's to know where everything lives so you can find it fast under pressure.

Common mistakes

✗ Not practicing under timed conditions. The time pressure is real.

✗ Skipping "boring" parts. Bonds, protests, and penalties are tested.

✗ Misreading questions. Watch for "EXCEPT," "NOT," and "LEAST" qualifiers.

✗ Poor tab organization. If you can't find it in 30 seconds, your tabs need work.

✗ Using outdated materials. Regulations change. Use current 19 CFR.

GRI Decision Flow

The 6 General Rules of Interpretation, applied in order. This is the legal framework for all classification. Source: General Notes to the HTSUS.

GRI 1

Headings, Section Notes, Chapter Notes

Classification determined by the terms of the headings and relative section/chapter notes. This resolves MOST questions. Start here always.

↓ only if GRI 1 doesn't resolve
GRI 2

Incomplete/unfinished articles; Mixtures and combinations

(a) Incomplete or unfinished articles classified as if complete, if they have the essential character. (b) Mixtures/combinations of materials - refer to GRI 3.

↓ only if GRI 2 doesn't resolve
GRI 3

Two or more headings apply

(a) Most specific heading preferred over general. (b) Mixtures/composites: essential character determines classification. (c) If (a) and (b) fail: last in numerical order.

↓ only if GRI 3 doesn't resolve
GRI 4

Most akin

Goods classified under the heading for goods to which they are most akin (most similar).

↓
GRI 5

Containers and packing

(a) Cases, boxes, and similar containers: classified with the article if of a kind normally sold with it. (b) Packing materials: classified with the article unless clearly reusable.

↓
GRI 6

Subheading classification

Classification at the subheading level follows the same rules (GRI 1-5) applied at that level. Compare only subheadings at the same level.

Valuation Methods Hierarchy

Applied in strict order. You can only move to the next method if the previous one cannot be used. Source: 19 CFR 152 Subpart E.

1

Transaction value

Price actually paid or payable, plus statutory additions (assists, royalties, packing, proceeds). This is the PRIMARY method - always try first.

§152.103
↓ only if transaction value can't be determined
2

Transaction value of identical merchandise

Same merchandise, same country of origin, same commercial level, approximately same time.

§152.104
↓
3

Transaction value of similar merchandise

Not identical but closely resembling in characteristics, materials, function. Same country, same time.

§152.104
↓ (Methods 4 & 5 can be applied in either order at importer's request)
4

Deductive value

Based on resale price in the U.S., minus deductions (commissions, transport, duties, profit).

§152.105
↓
5

Computed value

Built-up cost: materials + fabrication + profit + general expenses in country of production.

§152.106
↓ last resort
6

Fallback (derived from Methods 1-5)

Reasonable means consistent with the principles of Methods 1-5, applied flexibly. Cannot use: arbitrary values, minimum values, domestic selling prices, or "higher of two alternatives."

§152.107

Weird regulations that actually exist

🔪 The one-armed switchblade exception

19 CFR § 12.98: Switchblades are prohibited... unless you have only one arm. Then you can import one with a blade up to 3 inches.

The regulation specifically acknowledges utility for individuals with limited manual dexterity. This is real.

💍 Married women as sureties

Former 19 CFR § 113.35(b)(2): Used to state that a married woman could act as surety on a customs bond... unless her state prohibited it.

Removed to align with modern equal rights standards. But it was on the books for decades.

🚫 Felons need permission

19 CFR § 111.53(e): Customs brokers cannot employ anyone convicted of a felony without written approval from CBP's Assistant Commissioner.

HTS code oddities

🐵 You can import primates

HTS 0106.11: Live primates have their own classification. Good luck with the CITES permits and explaining the chimp to your neighbors.

🐋 Whale imports are classified

HTS 0106.12: Live whales, dolphins, and porpoises have their own code. Because someone thought we'd need it.

🚽 Toilet seats are plastic, not furniture

HTS 3924.90: Chapter 39 (plastics), not Chapter 94 (furniture). This has caused actual trade disputes. Classification matters for duty rates.

🏠 Prefab buildings are furniture

Chapter 94: Your shed, your garage, that modular home - all classified as "furniture." Chapter 94 is wild.

🐶 Dogs are "other mammals"

HTS 0106.19: Man's best friend gets lumped into "other live mammals not elsewhere specified." Primates and whales get their own codes. Dogs don't. Personally offended.
